Membership-based single-bay golf simulator side hustle.

Concrete side hustle blueprint: low-touch membership model that hit break-even in 3 months on 15 members and runs on $40/mo of software.

details

What it is: Jay Meldrum's *Swing Cave Golf* — a single-bay golf simulator run as a membership club. Opened September 2025, 28 members, 50% net margin, ~1 hr/week of owner time.

The thesis:

  • Home simulators sit idle 23 hrs/day — wasted capex on expensive square footage
  • Flip it: put one bay near your house, sell 24/7 access as memberships
  • Recurring revenue + near-zero ops once the door code works

How it works:

  • Format: One simulator bay, members-only, 24/7 keypad/door access
  • Validation: Talked to ~10 friends, 5 verbally committed, all 5 eventually joined — used as the floor to greenlight the buildout
  • Break-even math: 15 members covered all costs; hit at month 3 (mid-Sept → mid-Dec)
  • Current state: 28 members at month 6, zero churn, 50% net margin
  • Owner time: ~1 hour per week — no staff, no front desk
  • Tech stack: $40/mo scheduling software + smart-lock door access = entire ops stack
  • Buildout choice: Skipped the cheap fold-down home nets; commercial membership model justified going premium on the sim

Playbook to copy:

  • Pick a sticky hobby with high home-equipment cost and low daily utilization (golf, pickleball, podcast studio, music practice room, photo studio)
  • Pre-sell to 5–10 friends before signing a lease
  • Set member count to break-even, not to a revenue target — de-risks the launch
  • Use a friend as a beta member for product feedback (club straps, putting area, swing data views)
  • Automate access (smart lock) + scheduling so you're not the bottleneck

Tools & links:

  • Swing Cave Golf — the actual business profiled
  • Bizee — LLC formation (sponsor, but Chris uses it himself)
  • Scheduling SaaS at ~$40/mo (not named in transcript — likely Chronogolf, Skedda, or a sim-specific tool like foreUP)

Why it matters for you: This is the cleanest side-hustle template Chris has profiled in a while — small footprint, software-thin ops, recurring revenue, validated with 5 friends before any capex. Swap golf for whatever niche has the same "expensive at home, used 1 hr/day" profile and the math works.

15-Year-Old Sells $1 Bills for $15 On eBay

7.2K

Teen flips novelty dollar bills into $10K/day eBay business.

Dead-simple arbitrage proof — a 15-year-old with Office Max stickers built a five-figure-per-day eBay store, the kind of low-tech hustle that punches holes in the 'you need a stack' mindset.

details

Featured individual: Unnamed 15-year-old eBay seller (story shared by Shaan Puri / Sam Parr).

What it is: A teenager spotted novelty dollar bills selling for $15 on eBay (e.g., a real $1 bill with a Michael Jordan sticker covering George Washington), realized the production cost was near-zero, and turned it into the top-ranked custom novelty dollar bill store on the platform.

How he made the money — step by step:

  • Spotted the arbitrage: Saw existing listings of regular $1 bills with sticker overlays (Michael Jordan, etc.) selling for $15 a pop.
  • Reverse-engineered production: Realized the 'product' is just a sticker printed at Office Max slapped over Washington's face on a real dollar bill. Cost ≈ $1 bill + pennies of sticker paper.
  • Listed on eBay: Put them up for sale at ~$15 each.
  • Scaled volume: Averaged ~100 bills sold per day → ~$1,000/day in profit.
  • Grew to $10K/day in sales: Kept scaling listings and SKUs (different celebrity/character faces).
  • Won the category: Became the largest seller of custom novelty collectible dollar bills on eBay.
  • Compounded via search: Hitting top-ranked seller status boosted his organic placement inside eBay search, which fed more sales — a flywheel.

The unit economics (rough):

  • Cost of goods: ~$1 (the bill) + sticker (cents).
  • Sale price: ~$15.
  • Margin: ~$14/unit before eBay/PayPal fees and shipping.
  • Volume: 100/day → 670/day at peak (implied by the $10K/day sales figure).

Tools & links:

  • eBay — sales channel; ranking algorithm rewarded volume.
  • Office Max / OfficeMax — sticker paper printing for the overlays.

Why it matters for you: Pure case study in noticing a live arbitrage on a marketplace, replicating the cheapest version of the product, and letting the platform's own ranking system compound your distribution — no code, no funding, no team.
